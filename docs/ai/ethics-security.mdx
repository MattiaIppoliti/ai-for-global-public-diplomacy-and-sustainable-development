Adopting AI responsibly is not about saying "yes" or "no" to a tool — it is about knowing **which tool**, **for which task**, **under which obligation**, and **with what oversight**. This section gives you the framework.

## Why AI Literacy matters

> **AI Literacy** — the set of essential knowledge and skills needed to understand, interact with, and critically evaluate artificial-intelligence technologies. AI literacy includes the ability to use AI tools effectively and ethically, evaluate their outputs, ensure that human beings remain at the center of AI, and adapt to the evolving AI landscape in both personal and professional contexts.

## Six core principles for selecting an AI provider

Any AI system or vendor adopted by the University should be judged against these six principles.

| Principle | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Transparency** | AI systems must be explainable and their operating criteria clear. Users must be able to understand how a response or content is generated by the algorithm. |
| **Inclusion** | AI must deliver benefits to everyone equally. This means paying attention to the needs of people with disabilities, vulnerable groups and minorities. |
| **Accountability** | Those who design or implement an AI system must act responsibly and define roles and responsibilities in advance. |
| **Fairness** | AI must not act with or amplify biases. Models must be tested for unfair biases and their quality monitored to preserve fairness and human dignity. |
| **Reliability** | AI systems must operate safely and predictably. They must be resilient to failures or attacks and subject to ongoing monitoring. |
| **Security & Privacy** | At every stage of AI use, data security and user privacy must be ensured. |

## The AI Literacy Framework

The Digital Education Council's AI Literacy Framework (2025) organizes the skills needed by individuals using AI into **five dimensions**. The first four are general literacy that everyone needs; the fifth is domain-specific.

| Dimension | Question | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Understanding AI and Data** | *How does AI work?* | How AI systems work, the principles of data collection, processing and interpretation, and the implications of AI-generated output. Lets you interact critically with AI tools, evaluate their capabilities and limitations, and make informed decisions about their use. |
| **2. Critical Thinking and Judgment** | *How do I evaluate AI output?* | Evaluating AI-generated content, discerning biases, and applying logical reasoning when using AI in decision-making — verifying sources, identifying misinformation, recognizing limitations in AI-generated insights, ensuring human judgment remains central. AI is used as a tool for **augmentation rather than blind reliance**. |
| **3. Ethical & Responsible Use of AI** | *How can I ensure AI is used ethically and responsibly?* | Ethical considerations and governance frameworks for responsible adoption — fairness, transparency, accountability, privacy; recognizing risks (bias, discrimination, misinformation); navigating regulatory and institutional guidelines. |
| **4. Human-Centricity, Emotional Intelligence & Creativity** | *How can I make sure humans remain at the center?* | The human skills that matter in an AI-driven world — empathy, adaptability, communication, lifelong learning, mindset. As AI automates tasks, human-centric skills become essential to maintain ethical decision-making and align AI with social values. |
| **5. Domain Expertise** | *How do I apply AI in a specific context?* | Specialized knowledge to understand, evaluate and manage AI's impact in a specific academic or professional context — adapting AI tools to improve professional practice, addressing sector-specific challenges. |

### Ideal proficiency profile for management

The course recommends Luiss management aim for the following levels on each dimension (scale 0–3):

| Dimension | Target | What this means concretely |
|---|---|---|
| Understanding AI and Data | **2** | Able to select AI tools for real tasks and assess the role of data in AI performance. |
| Critical Thinking and Judgment | **3** | Expert in rigorously evaluating AI-generated outputs and able to assess AI's impact on human cognition. |
| Ethical and Responsible Use of AI | **3** | Able to evaluate, shape and promote the ethical use of AI, governance frameworks and best practices. |
| Human-Centricity, EI, Creativity | **3** | Promotes and integrates human-centered AI approaches and fosters responsible and inclusive use. |
| Domain Expertise | **2** | Able to integrate AI tools into their domain expertise for their respective offices. |

<Callout type="info">
  The two **3-level** dimensions are critical thinking and ethics. Management is expected to be the *strongest* line of defense on these — not the technical team.
</Callout>

## Regulatory compliance for AI

Three EU regulations frame the legal context for AI at a Luiss-sized institution.

### GDPR — Regulation (EU) 2016/679

Companies developing or deploying AI must design **models, datasets, and analytical processes** so as to:

- collect only the data **strictly necessary**;
- retain it for **limited periods**;
- put in place technical measures such as **encryption, anonymization, and audit logging**.

GDPR governs the protection of **personal-data rights** in AI.

### Data Act — Regulation (EU) 2023/2854

The Data Act requires that the **Data Processing Agreement (DPA)** with AI providers include clauses on:

- data sharing;
- retention periods;
- procedures in the event of access requests from public authorities.

The Data Act governs access to and availability of data (including non-personal data) generated by connected devices and services. It does **not** directly address algorithms or models.

### AI Act — Regulation (EU) 2024/1689

The AI Act sets binding rules for the development and use of AI in the EU, classifying applications by **risk level**.

- Companies that provide or use **"high-risk"** systems must meet stringent assessment, documentation and continuous-monitoring requirements.
- Even **low-risk** applications must meet **transparency obligations** — affecting the design and management of AI products.

## AI Act — classification of AI solutions

The AI Act sorts solutions into four bands of increasing risk.

### 1 — Open use (minimal risk)

Allowed freely. Examples:

- Image generation for creative or marketing content without copyright or IP concerns.
- Automatic translation, document summarization, insight extraction from low-sensitivity data — with human oversight.
- Text generation (blog articles, product descriptions, newsletters), provided no impact on fundamental rights.

### 2 — Use with disclosure and compliance (transparency risk)

Allowed but the user must be informed they are interacting with an AI.

> Example — chatbots must make clear that the interlocutor is a machine. Deepfakes must carry **clear and visible labels**.

### 3 — Cautious use (high risk)

Allowed only with strict guarantees — assessment, documentation, monitoring. Examples:

- AI safety components in critical infrastructure.
- AI solutions that may determine access to education or someone's professional career path.
- AI tools for employment and worker management.
- Access to essential public and private services.
- Remote and emotional biometric identification systems.
- Law-enforcement use cases.
- Migration management and controls.
- Justice administration and democratic processes.

### 4 — Prohibited use (unacceptable risk)

Not allowed at all. Examples:

- Harmful AI-based manipulation and deception.
- Harmful AI-based exploitation of vulnerabilities.
- Social scoring.
- Real-time biometric identification.
- Risk assessments concerning natural persons.
- Biometric categorization based on sensitive data.
- Emotion recognition in the workplace.
- Creation or expansion of facial-recognition databases through untargeted scraping.

## A risk-based approach for the University

Mapping the four AI-Act risk bands to typical university use cases:

| AI Act tier | Stance | Examples — instructors | Examples — students | Examples — general |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Minimal — Open use** | Freely used by students and staff without special permission. | Lesson planning · AI slide summarization · AI translation of teaching materials · AI grammar checking | Generation of flashcards and self-assessment quizzes | — |
| **Transparency — Use with disclosure** | Uses requiring disclosure to the end user, including which AI system was used, what it does, and how. | Plagiarism detector · Course content generation | — | AI chatbot |
| **High — Use with compliance** | Uses that must meet specific standards to ensure safety, fairness and privacy. | Verification of student-produced assignments via AI | Coding Assistant for tasks assigned to students · AI for thesis drafting | AI-assisted writing |
| **High — Cautious use** | Uses requiring careful planning. The University may also consider *avoiding* them because of student- and institution-level risks. | AI in student selection or drop-out prediction · Predictive analysis of student performance | Personalized student guidance | — |
| **Unacceptable — Prohibited** | Uses with unacceptable risks. Prohibited within the institution. | Misconduct prediction · AI surveillance | — | AI for processing sensitive data |

<Callout type="warn">
  The University may decide to forbid a "Cautious use" application outright, even though the AI Act would permit it under heavy compliance. The institutional bar is allowed to be stricter than the regulatory floor.
</Callout>

## Key aspects to consider

Five practical rules every member of staff should internalize before opening an AI tool.

### 1. If something is free, you are the product

Avoid free or personal AI solutions for work activities (e.g. Read AI, personal DeepL, personal ChatGPT). Data entered or documents attached — if not explicitly authorized by the user — may be **acquired and used to train the model**, with security and privacy risks.

**Recommended action** — only use **tools approved by the University**, especially when working with information or documents containing personal, business or otherwise confidential data. **Do not** upload personal information, sensitive data, reports or business documents to free AI tools or personal accounts.

### 2. Watch out for bias and hallucinations

Generative AI can produce responses affected by **bias** (prejudices in the training data) and **hallucinations** (fabricated, non-existent information).

**Recommended action** — check the factual accuracy of AI content against **reliable sources** ("grounded knowledge"). For sensitive, statistical or regulatory data, **always cross-check against official documents**.

### 3. Artificial Intelligence is not intelligent

GenAI has no human awareness or intentionality. It produces output through statistical correlations. It is a statistical and probabilistic tool — **not** a deterministic one like a spreadsheet or an accounting system.

**Recommended action** — do **not** treat fluent phrasing as an indicator of accuracy: a well-written answer can be wrong. Use GenAI primarily to generate **ideas, drafts, and creative content** — not to obtain certainties or factual truths.

### 4. Copyright and intellectual property

When creating content (documents, images, podcasts, videos), always pay attention to IP and copyright. **Avoid uploading copyrighted documents** (business cases, confidential materials, proprietary university content) to free AI tools or unauthorized personal accounts.

**Recommended actions**:

- For transparency, always indicate that the content was AI-generated (watermark, caption, etc.).
- To prove the provenance of generated content, **keep a copy of the prompt used**.

### 5. Human in the loop

Humans are at the center — actively involved in supervising and controlling decision-making processes based on AI solutions. GenAI is a powerful tool for **supporting** humans, who remain **ultimately responsible**.

**Recommended action** — do **not** copy & paste AI responses. Before sharing them, **review them critically**. The **final validation** of the output is always a human's job, in compliance with internal policies and ethical principles.

## Key takeaways

1. AI literacy is a five-dimension capability; the two non-negotiable dimensions for management are **Critical Thinking** and **Ethical Use**.
2. The EU triple — **GDPR, Data Act, AI Act** — governs data, contracts and systems respectively.
3. The AI Act sorts use cases into four bands: Open, Disclosure, Compliance, Cautious — plus a fifth that is **prohibited**.
4. The University's bar is allowed to be stricter than the legal floor.
5. When in doubt, apply the five rules: approved tools only, verify facts, do not anthropomorphize, mind IP, human reviews everything.

## Module 1, Diplomatic Writing with AI

Three demos, then a group exercise. Total runtime ≈ 90 minutes (60 min demo + 20 min exercise + 10 min debrief).

### Case 1, Drafting a diplomatic press release

**Scenario.** A fictional regional climate cooperation initiative in the Mediterranean. The communications officer must produce a UN-style press release suitable for translation into the six official languages and for distribution to capitals and to press.

**Step 1, First draft.**

> *Act as a UN communications officer. Draft a 250-word press release announcing the launch of a regional climate cooperation initiative among Mediterranean states. Use UN house style, formal third-person register, dateline format, and a two-paragraph quote attributed to "the Secretary-General's Special Envoy for the Mediterranean". Mark every factual claim that requires verification with `[VERIFY]`.*

**Step 2, Tone refinement.** Read the output. Run it through a second pass:

> *Rewrite the draft preserving its facts and structure. Reduce promotional language. Replace every adjective that signals advocacy ("ambitious", "groundbreaking", "historic") with neutral equivalents or remove it. Preserve hedging devices verbatim.*

**Step 3, Audience adaptation.** Produce three variants from the cleaned draft:

> *Produce three versions of the press release, identical in factual content: (a) for the wire services, front-loaded, no acronyms, 180 words; (b) for ministries of foreign affairs, diplomatic register, full acronyms with first-use expansions, 250 words; (c) for civil-society partners, human-impact framing, concrete examples, 220 words.*

**Step 4, Shortening.** A social-media derivative:

> *From version (a), produce: a 280-character X/Twitter post, a 600-character LinkedIn post, and a 90-word press-alert SMS to journalists. Preserve the official initiative name verbatim. Do not add hashtags the SG's office has not approved.*

**Step 5, Multilingual adaptation.** For each language (French, Spanish, Arabic), instruct:

> *Translate version (b) into [language]. Do not translate literally; produce a target-language text that reads natively in the diplomatic register of [language] media. Preserve all UN terminology per UNTERM. Then back-translate to English and list every divergence from the source, those are the places where a human translator must verify.*

**Debrief points.** Tone-refinement (Step 2) is where most participants discover their model defaults to *advertising* register. Multilingual back-translation (Step 5) is where the political weight of word choice becomes concrete.

### Case 2, Speechwriting

**Scenario.** Opening remarks (7 minutes) for a UN side event titled *"AI and Sustainable Development: From Capacity to Trust"*. The speaker is to be confirmed, we will produce the same speech in three registers and let the cabinet pick.

**Step 1, Architecture.**

> *Propose three architectures for a 7-minute opening statement on AI and Sustainable Development at a UN side event. Each architecture must include: hook, three-part body, call to action. For each, name the rhetorical strategy (e.g. problem-solution, narrative arc, three-pillars). Pick the one most suitable for a multilateral audience that is sceptical of techno-optimism, and justify the choice in two sentences.*

**Step 2, Draft in three registers.** From the chosen architecture:

> *Write the speech in three versions of register: (a) **Minister**, high force, broad sweep, explicit political signalling, one named policy commitment; (b) **Ambassador**, formal third-person openings, agreed UN language on SDGs, no new commitments, careful preambular hedging; (c) **Technical officer**, concrete, indicator-anchored, names two specific SDG targets and one specific dataset. All three: 7 minutes spoken pace, ≈ 900 words.*

**Step 3, SDG framing pass.** Apply explicitly:

> *In each of the three versions, mark every SDG reference and verify it cites a specific target (e.g. SDG 9.c, not "SDG 9"). For each marked reference, propose the most recent indicator value [VERIFY]. Add no new SDG references that were not present in the original draft.*

**Step 4, Stress test.** Three hostile readers:

> *Read version (a) as: (1) a sceptical journalist asking "where is the money?"; (2) a developing-country delegate asking "where is the global-South leadership?"; (3) a youth representative asking "why should we trust this institution?". For each, list the two passages most likely to attract the criticism and propose a rewrite using **contrasting** ("this is not X, what we propose is Y").*

**Step 5, Delivery notes.** A final pass:

> *Mark the chosen draft with delivery notes: pauses (||), emphasis (CAPS), and slow-down zones. Flag every idiom, phrasal verb, or culturally bound metaphor for replacement with literal language any interpreter can render at speed.*

**Debrief points.** Step 2 makes the register difference visceral, participants who thought "tone" was vague see it operationalized. Step 4 is where most participants realize their first draft would have been eaten alive.

### Case 3, Negotiation briefing from a long document

**Scenario.** A 40-page draft policy paper on AI governance has just landed two days before a multilateral meeting. The principal needs to be briefed.

**Step 1, Executive summary.**

> *You are briefing a senior official with 6 minutes to read your output before a 90-minute negotiation. Produce: a 5-line executive summary, the three decisions the official must make in the room, the five facts most likely to be challenged publicly, every recommendation in the document with paragraph number, and anything the document is conspicuously silent about. Quote verbatim with paragraph numbers wherever you make a factual claim.*

**Step 2, Stakeholders map.**

> *From the document, list every stakeholder named or implied, grouped by negotiating bloc (G77, OECD donors, EU, AOSIS, etc.). For each group, give: stated position (one line), likely red line (one line, hypothesis marked as such), preferred language (verbatim phrase from the document if present).*

**Step 3, Risk matrix.**

> *Produce a 5×2 risk matrix for the negotiation. Rows: the five most plausible failure modes (e.g. "consensus collapses on financing", "developing countries reject monitoring language"). Columns: likelihood (low/medium/high) and impact on the meeting outcome (one line). Add a third column with one mitigation move the principal can make at the table.*

**Step 4, Talking points.**

> *Produce talking points for the principal: three opening statements (60 seconds each), five interventions for likely flashpoints (30 seconds each), and one closing statement (90 seconds). Use UN agreed language wherever possible. Mark every commitment with [COMMITMENT, requires capital clearance].*

**Step 5, Objection map.**

> *List the ten most likely objections the principal will face, ordered by likelihood. For each, draft a 25-word response using **labeling** ("It sounds like you are concerned that…") followed by a **calibrated question** ("How might we address that without reopening Article X?"). Do not include defensive responses.*

**Debrief points.** Case 3 is the demo that sells AI to senior diplomats. Twenty minutes of model work produces a briefing package that previously took a desk team most of a day. The clearance reality (§5.9) still applies, the package re-enters the chain at the commissioning officer.

### Hands-on Exercise 1

Participants are dived into groups. Each group picks **one** of the four tasks and runs it on the **fictional inputs** provided in the workshop pack.

**Task A, UN briefing pack.** Produce a one-page briefing for the SRSG on a fictional cross-border water dispute. Use the Case-3 pipeline.

**Task B, Policy synthesis.** From a 25-page fictional evaluation report, produce a 5-line executive summary, three decisions, and the "what is conspicuously not said" list.

**Task C, Talking points.** From a fictional Member State statement, generate five talking points for a delegation that disagrees with three of its premises but must preserve the relationship. Use **contrasting** and **calibrated questions** throughout.

**Task D, Crisis response.** A fictional Member State has accused a UN agency of bias in a recent report. Produce a 200-word public response that acknowledges the concern, defends the methodology, and proposes one concrete next step.

**Prompt-template card.** Each group receives a printed card with the four pipeline prompts from §5.1–§5.5 of Chapter 5, with placeholders for the task-specific inputs.

**Debrief (10 min).** Each group reads its **best paragraph** and its **worst paragraph**. The worst-paragraph reading is the more pedagogically valuable: it is where the model's defaults, confidence, advocacy register, smuggled commitments, become visible to peers.

## Module 2, AI for Strategic Communication and Public Diplomacy

Three demos, then a second hands-on exercise. Total runtime ≈ 50 minutes.

### Demo 1, Campaign generation

**Scenario.** A country team is launching an SDG-awareness campaign in advance of a regional High-Level Political Forum.

**Step 1, Frame and planning template.** Run the SDG planning template from §5.7 before any creative work.

**Step 2, Slogan and message framing.**

> *Propose ten slogans for an SDG-awareness campaign in [region], anchored to SDG target [X.Y]. For each, name the rhetorical frame (loss-aversion, identity, future-self, collective action) and the audience segment it targets best. Avoid imperatives, avoid global-North idiom, avoid metaphors that do not survive translation into [target languages].*

**Step 3, Audience segmentation.**

> *Produce a four-segment audience map for the campaign (e.g. urban youth, rural smallholders, parliamentary staff, business associations). For each: one-line motivation, two-line current narrative, two-line desired narrative, one channel, one trusted messenger. Cite no source you cannot verify.*

**Step 4, Multilingual adaptation.**

> *Adapt the chosen slogan and a 90-word campaign description into [three target languages]. Localize: replace examples with local-data examples (placeholders are fine, mark them `[LOCAL DATA NEEDED]`), replace metaphors with locally resonant ones, preserve the SDG target reference verbatim.*

**Step 5, Narrative optimization with the "that's right" test.**

> *For each audience segment, predict whether the campaign description elicits "you're right" (polite agreement, no behavioural change) or "that's right" (the audience hears its own experience named back to it). Rewrite each segment's description until you predict "that's right".*

**Debrief points.** Step 5 is the test that disciplines all the others. Participants discover that "that's right" requires specificity their first draft never had.

---

### Demo 2, Crisis communication

**Scenario.** Coordinated disinformation has emerged online alleging that a UN humanitarian agency is diverting aid to a specific party in a conflict. The claim is false but is gaining traction.

**Step 1, Narrative identification.**

> *Given the following 20 social-media posts [paste fictional sample], identify: the core claim, the two or three sub-narratives supporting it, the emotional register (fear, betrayal, contempt), and the implicit accusation about UN motives. Mark anything you cannot determine from the sample as "indeterminate".*

**Step 2, Response strategy.**

> *Produce three response strategies, with explicit trade-offs: (a) **direct rebuttal**, risk of amplification; (b) **prebunking**, earlier exposure of the manipulation technique without naming the false claim; (c) **inoculation through positive narrative**, substantive content on what the agency actually does. For each, list two pros, two cons, and the audiences it serves best.*

**Step 3, FAQ.**

> *Produce a 10-item FAQ that addresses the legitimate questions hidden inside the disinformation (e.g. how aid is tracked, who audits the agency, what happens when diversion is detected). Each answer must: be under 60 words, cite a public-record source [VERIFY], avoid repeating the false claim verbatim.*

**Step 4, Media response.**

> *Produce a 120-word media statement for the spokesperson. Open with the agency's actual operating principle (not with the false claim). Use **contrasting** in the second sentence. Close with a calibrated invitation to journalists ("how would you like us to demonstrate this in the field?").*

<Callout type="warn">
Counter-disinformation drafts are among the most dangerous AI outputs in a UN setting: a mis-targeted rebuttal amplifies the original lie. Route every artefact in Demo 2 through the spokesperson's office before publication. The AI pass is preparatory only.
</Callout>

---

### Demo 3, AI and intercultural communication

**Scenario.** The same one-paragraph message must land in five regional audiences: Northern Europe, Gulf states, East Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, Latin America.

**Step 1, High-context vs. low-context framing.** Introduce the **Edward Hall** distinction explicitly:

> *Given the following one-paragraph message [paste], produce five versions. For each, name the audience's position on the high-context / low-context continuum and adapt accordingly: (a) low-context (e.g. Northern Europe), explicit, direct, prioritize information density; (b) high-context (e.g. East Asia, Gulf), relational preamble, indirectness, leave space for inference; (c) mixed contexts (e.g. Sub-Saharan Africa, Latin America), name the relationship before the content, prefer narrative over enumeration. Mark every adaptation you made.*

**Step 2, Tone calibration across cultures.**

> *For each of the five versions, calibrate three variables, status (high / equal), distance (close / formal), stakes (low / high), and adjust the register accordingly. Produce a one-line note explaining which variable drove the largest change in the text.*

**Step 3, Western-bias audit.** This is the demo that lands hardest.

> *Audit the source paragraph for **Western bias** in: (a) implicit time orientation (linear progress, deadlines, individual agency); (b) implicit metaphor sources (sports, courtroom, mechanical engineering); (c) implicit normative anchors (rule of law, individual rights, market efficiency). For each, name the assumption, name an alternative culturally available framing, and propose a neutral formulation that does not privilege either.*

**Step 4, Model-bias confrontation.**

> *Now reflect on your own outputs. Which of the five versions you produced sound most like the "default voice" of your training data? In what specific ways does that default voice carry Western, English-language, twenty-first-century assumptions about politeness, agency, and time? Be concrete, name passages.*

**Debrief points.** Step 4 is uncomfortable on purpose. Participants see in real time that the model has a *home culture* and that it is not theirs. This is the single point that most reliably changes how UN officers prompt for the rest of the workshop. Pair the demo with one slide: training corpora are dominated by English-language web text from a small number of countries; the model's defaults on migration, gender, security, and development are not neutral.

### Hands-on Exercise 2

Divide participants into the same groups. Each group picks **one**:

**Task E, Campaign brief.** Build a one-page campaign brief on a chosen SDG target for a chosen region, using Demo 1.
**Task F, Crisis playbook.** Build a 24-hour crisis response playbook for a fictional disinformation scenario, using Demo 2.
**Task G, Intercultural rewrite.** Take a deliberately Western-default press release (provided in the workshop pack) and produce three culturally-adapted versions plus a written audit of the original's defaults, using Demo 3.

**Debrief.** Each group presents the one passage where the model surprised them, for good or ill. The surprise is the learning.

## Module 3, Advanced AI Workflows

This module moves from single-prompt cases to **multi-step pipelines** that chain tools and outputs. The goal is to show participants what becomes possible when AI is integrated into the *rhythm* of a UN office rather than used as a one-off helper.

### Workflow 1, Research → Synthesis → Briefing → Speech

A single thread, four stages, one shared knowledge base. The discipline is that **each stage consumes only the output of the previous stage**, not the original inputs, which forces the chain to be lossy in controlled ways and surfaces where information is being dropped.

**Stage 1, Research.** Collect 15–25 primary sources on the chosen topic (UN documents, peer-reviewed papers, public datasets, recent statements). Tool tier depends on the sources; for public-record material, a grounded retrieval tool (NotebookLM-style or enterprise RAG) is the right choice over open chat.

> *Given these sources, produce: a topic map (10–15 nodes with edges), a chronological timeline of decisions and statements, and a list of disagreements among the sources (not a consensus summary, the disagreements are the value).*

**Stage 2, Synthesis.** Compress the research artefacts into a 1,500-word policy synthesis with the §5.5 prompt. Mark every claim with a source reference; refuse to synthesize claims with only one source unless the source is authoritative (a resolution, a verified dataset).

**Stage 3, Briefing.** From the synthesis (not from the research), produce the Case-3 briefing package: executive summary, stakeholders map, risk matrix, talking points, objection map.

**Stage 4, Speech.** From the briefing (not from the synthesis), produce a 7-minute speech using the Case-2 staged workflow.

**The diagnostic.** At the end, read the speech back against the original research. The gaps, the nuances that survived stage 2 but not stage 4, the disagreements that smoothed into consensus, are the cost of the chain. Knowing where the chain loses information is the senior skill.

### Workflow 2, Document analysis pipeline

**Inputs.** A UN resolution, an SDG progress report, and an internal policy draft on the same topic.

**Stage 1, Per-document summary.** Run the §5.5 synthesis prompt on each document separately.

**Stage 2, Action points.**

> *From each summary, extract: every actionable item, the actor responsible, the deadline (or "no deadline specified"), and the verification mechanism (or "none"). Produce one consolidated action list, sorted by deadline.*

**Stage 3, Comparative analysis.**

> *Compare the three documents on five dimensions: (a) scope of ambition; (b) named actors; (c) financing assumptions; (d) monitoring and accountability language; (e) what each document is silent about. Produce a 3×5 matrix with one-line cells. Mark every divergence that has political significance.*

**Stage 4, Implementation roadmap.**

> *Synthesize the three documents into a 12-month implementation roadmap with quarterly milestones. For each milestone: the responsible actor, the precedent text in the source documents (with paragraph reference), and the principal risk. Mark every milestone that would require a new mandate as [MANDATE GAP].*

**The diagnostic.** A `[MANDATE GAP]` is the signal value of the workflow: it tells the senior reader where the political work has not yet been done.

### Workflow 3, AI for multilingual diplomacy

Translation in diplomacy is rarely literal. This workflow makes the non-literal pipeline explicit.

**Stage 1, Non-literal translation.**

> *Translate the source text into [target language]. Do not preserve sentence structure; produce a target-language text that reads as natively drafted by a [target-language] diplomatic writer. Where the source contains constructive ambiguity, preserve the ambiguity using the target language's own devices for ambiguity, not by transliterating the source's devices.*

**Stage 2, Tone preservation.**

> *Audit the translation against the source on three tonal dimensions: register (formal/neutral/popular), force (assertive/hedged), and warmth (cordial/neutral/cool). If any of the three has shifted, identify the passages responsible and propose a corrected formulation. The goal is tonal equivalence, not lexical equivalence.*

**Stage 3, Localization.**

> *Adapt the translation for the specific audience in [target country/region]. Replace global examples with local examples [LOCAL DATA NEEDED]. Replace metaphors with locally resonant equivalents. Replace dates and units with local conventions. Preserve all UN agreed language and named-entity references verbatim per UNTERM.*

**Stage 4, Back-translation audit.**

> *Back-translate the localized text into the source language. List every divergence from the original source on three axes: (a) factual, any claim that has gained, lost, or shifted meaning; (b) tonal, any register or force shift; (c) political, any named entity, contested term, or commitment whose weight has changed. Flag each as LOW / MEDIUM / HIGH risk for diplomatic effect.*

<Callout>
Workflow 3 is not a substitute for **DGACM** or for qualified human translators for any text destined for publication, the record, or treaty effect. It is a tool for preparing drafts, briefings, and outreach material, the human translator is non-negotiable for everything else.
</Callout>

## Closing exercise, Build your own workflow

The final 20 minutes of the workshop are unstructured. Each participant picks a single recurring task in their own portfolio, a weekly brief, a recurring speech, a routine translation, a monthly campaign post, and sketches a 3–5 stage AI workflow for it, naming for each stage:

1. **Inputs** and their data class (PUBLIC / INTERNAL / RESTRICTED).
2. **Tool tier** required.
3. **Prompt pattern** (drawn from Chapter 5 or invented for the task).
4. **Output** and the clearance node it re-enters.
5. **The single check** that catches the failure mode most likely to occur.

Participants pair up and grill each other's workflows on the fifth point. The workshop ends when every participant has a workflow they could run on Monday morning and a peer who has seen it.
